Lesson 1: Foundations

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will:

  • Understand why AI video generation requires a different workflow than traditional filmmaking
  • Identify the visual continuity problems inherent in AI video generation
  • Create a high-quality master image suitable for multi-angle scene construction
  • Evaluate what makes a master image effective or ineffective

Part 1: The Problem

Opening Exercise: "The Continuity Disaster"

‚ùå The Wrong Way

Each clip generated independently:

  • Shot 1: "Two people on a park bench at sunset"
  • Shot 2: "Close-up of woman in red dress"
  • Shot 3: "Man responds angrily"

Issues:

  • Woman's dress color changed
  • Man's hair is different
  • Bench style shifted
  • Lighting inconsistent

‚úì The Right Way

Using the Master Image Method:

  • All shots from ONE source image
  • Perfect continuity maintained
  • Characters identical
  • Wardrobe consistent
  • Location unchanged

Result:

Professional, editable scene coverage!

Discussion Questions:

  • What did you notice about the first example?
  • Why can't we just describe everything perfectly in each prompt?
  • How is this different from traditional filmmaking where continuity is controlled on set?
"In traditional film, continuity lives on set.
In AI film, continuity lives in the source image."

Part 2: Understanding the Master Image

What Makes a Good Master Image?

The Non-Negotiables:

  • Spatial Clarity: All key characters visible with clear spatial relationships
  • Wide Enough: Room for the camera to "move" to different angles
  • Narrative Information: The setting tells us something about the scene
  • Lighting Consistency: Lighting that can work for multiple angles
  • Character Definition: Faces, costumes, and key props clearly visible

‚úì GOOD Example:

"Cinematic wide shot, two adults sitting on opposite ends of a wooden park bench at golden hour sunset, man on left in blue button-down shirt and jeans, woman on right in red sundress, oak trees in background, natural lighting, photorealistic"

Why it works:

‚úì Both characters fully visible ‚úì Clear left/right positioning ‚úì Specific wardrobe details ‚úì Defined setting ‚úì Room to reframe to either character

‚ùå POOR Example:

"Two people talking, dramatic lighting"

Why it fails:

‚úó Too vague ‚úó No spatial information ‚úó No character details ‚úó Problematic lighting

Part 3: Creating Your Master Image

Step-by-Step Process:

  • Start with Your Scene Concept
    • What's your scene about?
    • Who are your characters?
    • Where does it take place?
  • Write Your Master Image Prompt
  • Generate and Evaluate

    • Prompt Structure Template:

    [Shot Type], [Number] [character descriptions] [doing what] in/at [location], [specific wardrobe details], [lighting type], [style notes]

    Example Student Exercise:

    Scene: A job interview going badly

    "Cinematic medium-wide shot, nervous young woman in gray blazer sitting across desk from stern older man in dark suit, modern office with glass windows showing city skyline, fluorescent overhead lighting, corporate photography style, sharp focus"

    Common First-Attempt Problems:

    • Characters too close together (hard to get clean OTS)
    • Faces obscured or turned away
    • Cluttered backgrounds that might cause issues
    • Extreme angles that limit coverage options

    Part 4: Student Work Time

    Assignment: Create Your Master Image

    Requirements:

    1. Write a 2-3 sentence scene description (what happens in your scene)
    2. Draft your master image prompt using the template
    3. Generate at least 3 variations
    4. Select your final master image
    5. Submit with a brief explanation (50 words) of why you chose this image

    Evaluation Criteria:

    20%

    Both/all characters clearly visible

    20%

    Spatial relationships obvious

    20%

    Sufficient framing space for coverage

    20%

    Consistent, workable lighting

    20%

    Narrative clarity

    Instructor Circulates:

    • Help students refine prompts
    • Point out potential issues before generation
    • Encourage specificity over vagueness
    • Remind: "This image is your foundation for everything"

    Part 5: Gallery Critique

    Questions to Ask for Each Image:

    • What works about this image?
    • What coverage angles could you get from this?
    • What potential problems do you see?
    • How would you improve the prompt?
    Key Takeaways
    • Specificity is your friend ‚Äì Vague prompts = unpredictable results
    • Think like a cinematographer ‚Äì You need room to move the camera
    • This is your foundation ‚Äì Everything else builds from this
    • Iteration is normal ‚Äì Professional filmmakers do multiple takes; you'll do multiple generations
