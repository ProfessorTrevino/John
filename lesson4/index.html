The Philosophy of Coverage Editing

üé¨

Principle #1: The Master Establishes

Use the master shot to establish geography. Start your scene with the master so the audience knows where everyone is. Return to it when spatial relationships might be confusing.

üí¨

Principle #2: OTS for Conversation

Over-the-shoulder shots are the workhorses of dialogue. They show who's talking, who's listening, and maintain screen direction. Use them for the "normal" flow of conversation.

üò¢

Principle #3: Close-Ups for Emotion

Close-ups are your emotional amplifiers. Use them sparingly for maximum impact. Save them for the most important lines, realizations, or reactions.

‚ö°

Principle #4: Cut for Reason

Every cut should have a purpose. Cut to show a reaction, emphasize a line, reveal new information, or change the energy of the scene. Never cut just because you can.

Step 1: Timeline Setup

Organize Your Timeline

A well-organized timeline makes editing faster and more creative. Here's the recommended setup:

VIDEO TRACK 4: [Close-Ups] VIDEO TRACK 3: [OTS Shots] VIDEO TRACK 2: [Master Shot - Reference] VIDEO TRACK 1: [Active Edit] AUDIO TRACK 1: [Dialogue from best angle] AUDIO TRACK 2: [Music/SFX]

Track-by-Track Breakdown:

  • V4 - Close-Ups: Keep all CU segments here, ready to drop in
  • V3 - OTS Shots: Both OTS angles organized and accessible
  • V2 - Master Shot Reference: Full master sequence, locked, for timing reference
  • V1 - Active Edit: Your actual cut‚Äîstart with master, then replace/layer
  • A1 - Dialogue: Extract best audio, sync all angles to this
  • A2 - Music/SFX: Additional audio elements

üí° Workflow Tip

Lock your master shot on V2. This prevents you from accidentally moving it and gives you a constant reference for timing. You can always glance up to see where you are in the scene.

Rhythm & Pacing

Editing is music‚Äîit's all about rhythm

Shot Duration Creates Feeling

Long Holds (5+ seconds)

Create contemplation, tension, weight. Use for emotional moments where you want the audience to sit with a character.

Example: Hold on a character's face as they process devastating news.

Medium Beats (2-4 seconds)

The standard conversational rhythm. Comfortable pacing that feels natural for most dialogue.

Example: OTS shots cutting back and forth in a normal conversation.

Quick Cuts (1-2 seconds)

Generate energy, urgency, intensity. Use sparingly for arguments, revelations, or heightened emotion.

Example: Rapid cutting between faces during an argument.

Some of your most powerful moments will have no dialogue. Don't be afraid to hold on a reaction shot in silence. Let the audience feel the emotion.

Solving Common Problems

üîß Problem-Solving in the Edit

AI-generated footage will have imperfections. Here's how to work around them:

Lip-Sync is Off
Solution: Use reaction shots where the character isn't speaking. Cut to the listener, or use the master where lip-sync isn't as noticeable. If possible, adjust audio timing by a few frames.
Dialogue Doesn't Match
Solution: Pick the BEST audio from one angle and use it for all angles. Sync all video angles to this single audio track. This guarantees perfect sync.
Character Looks Different
Solution: Use that angle less. Don't linger on shots where continuity is broken. Quick cuts help hide small differences.
Jarring Transition
Solution: Add a brief cutaway (reaction shot, insert, or return to master) to smooth the transition. The cutaway acts as a visual "palate cleanser."
Awkward Pause/Gap
Solution: Use a reaction shot during the pause, or cut to the master to let the moment breathe. Don't rush to fill silence.
Not Enough Coverage
Solution: Use the master shot more. If you're short on close-ups or OTS shots, the master can carry the scene. It's your safety net.
Too Much Coverage
Solution: Be selective. Just because you generated an angle doesn't mean you need to use it. Edit ruthlessly‚Äîonly include what serves the story.

üí° Pro Editor Trick: The L-Cut and J-Cut

These are your secret weapons for smooth transitions:

  • L-Cut: Audio from Shot A continues into Shot B (visual cuts, audio lags)
  • J-Cut: Audio from Shot B starts before we see Shot B (audio leads, visual follows)

These techniques make cuts feel more natural and help with sync issues.
