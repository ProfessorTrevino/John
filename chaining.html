The "Chaining Coverage" Method

How to Actually Make a Movie with AI

By: Professor John Treviño, MFA | UTRGV

The Core Idea

AI creates clips, not scenes.

  • Current AI video tools give you 7-second clips
  • Characters change shot-to-shot
  • Lighting and framing don't match
  • You can't cut them into a real scene

❌ The Problem

Every new prompt creates a completely different world

✓ The Solution

Chaining Coverage:
→ Make 1 Master Frame
→ Tell the AI to move the camera from that single image
→ All shots now match perfectly

The Core Problem

Why AI Can't Make a Movie (Yet)

  • Each prompt = a completely different world
  • A "two-shot" and a "close-up" look like different movies
  • Actor changes
  • Wardrobe changes
  • Room changes
  • Light changes
→ Impossible to edit
→ You end up with separate 7-second islands, not a narrative

What Filmmakers Need

Filmmaking requires:

  • Matching continuity
  • Matching actors
  • Matching lighting
  • Matching geography
AI video fails because it resets the universe every time.
Chaining Coverage forces the AI to stay consistent.

The Insight

Filmmaking = Coverage

Coverage = Wide → Medium → Close-up → Over-the-Shoulder

AI only fails because we ask for each one separately.

Fix: Start with one picture, then direct the AI like a camera operator.

The 3-Step Method

1

Create Your Master Frame

The Master Frame locks: Actors, Wardrobe, Lighting, Location, Blocking

This becomes the visual DNA for the entire scene.

2

Feed the Master Frame Back In

Upload the Master Frame into the AI

Do NOT describe a new shot

Do NOT ask it to recreate the scene

Force the AI to reuse that exact image as its foundation

3

Tell the AI to "Move the Camera"

Examples:

  • • "From this picture, give me an OTS on the left character"
  • • "From this picture, zoom to a medium shot on the woman"
  • • "From this picture, dolly in for a close-up on the man"

Resulting Coverage

From one picture, you generate:

  • Two-shot
  • OTS left
  • OTS right
  • Medium left
  • Medium right
  • Close-up left
  • Close-up right
This is real, usable coverage.

Two Ways to Use the Method

1. Realistic Workflow

Make one Master Frame for the whole scene, generate all coverage in one batch

Faster
Great for full scenes
Good continuity

2. "Perfect" Workflow

New Master Frame for each line of dialogue (Per-Sentence Method)

Line-by-line precision
Best shot control
Best emotional continuity
More work
Choose based on your purpose: speed vs. control

Why This Works

  • You become the director again
  • AI becomes the camera operator
  • You decide: Shots, Angles, Emotional beats
  • AI gives you the frames, you make the movie
This restores the filmmaking process.

What Students Learned

  • AI isn't replacing filmmakers
  • AI without direction is dumb
  • A director's job is choosing shots
  • Chaining Coverage puts humans back in control
Students stopped fearing AI and started using it like a real tool.

Future of AI Filmmaking

AI companies should build: "Scene Mode"

  • Create one Master Frame
  • Auto-generate every shot from it
  • Provide real, editable coverage
Until then, this method is the blueprint.

Final Takeaway

This is how you stop getting random 7-second clips and start making real movies with AI:

1. Make a Master Frame

2. Feed it back

3. Tell the AI to move the camera

This is the fix.
This is Chaining Coverage.

Assignment: Chaining Coverage in AI Filmmaking

Chaining Coverage

Making a whole scene from ONE picture

What You're Doing

You will create a full set of movie shots — all matching — starting from one main picture.

Think of it like this:
You take one master photo, and then you "move the camera" around inside AI to get the rest of the shots.

Why This Matters

Editors can only cut a scene together if all the shots match. If the face changes, the clothes change, or the lighting changes, the whole scene falls apart.

This assignment teaches you how to keep everything consistent.

✅ What You Must Turn In (7 pictures total)

1. Master Two-Shot

(both characters in the frame)

master_twoshot.png
2. OTS A→B

(over-the-shoulder, looking at character B)

OTS_A_to_B.png
3. OTS B→A

(reverse angle)

OTS_B_to_A.png
4. Medium Shot A MS_A.png
5. Medium Shot B MS_B.png
6. Close-Up A CU_A.png
7. Close-Up B CU_B.png
Put them all in one folder with your name.

✅ How to Do It (Easy Steps)

1. Make the Master Two-Shot

  • Two characters standing or sitting
  • Clear faces
  • Simple background
  • Good lighting

This picture is the anchor. Everything else must look like it came from this.

2. Make the OTS Shots

From the master image:

  • Move the camera behind Character A, looking at Character B → OTS A→B
  • Then do the opposite → OTS B→A

Keep:

  • Same faces
  • Same clothes
  • Same lighting
  • Same background

3. Make the Medium Shots

Push the camera closer:

  • Waist to chest level
  • Same person, same look, same lighting

4. Make the Close-Ups

Move the camera closer again:

  • Head and shoulders
  • Eyes sharp
  • Face exactly the same as before

✅ What MUST Stay the Same

This is the entire point of the assignment:
✓ Same face
✓ Same hair
✓ Same clothes
✓ Same lighting
✓ Same background
✓ Same style
If anything changes — the scene breaks.

✅ Four-Day Schedule

Day 1:

Make the master two-shot.

Day 2:

Make both OTS shots.

Day 3:

Make both medium shots.

Day 4:

Make both close-ups and double-check everything matches.

✅ Grading (Simple)

Total: 100 points
All 7 shots complete 30 pts
Everything matches visually 40 pts
Clean, organized work 15 pts
Correct file names 15 pts

✅ Tips to Make Your Life Easier

  • Use image-to-image (don't start over each time)
  • Use face lock or keep character if your AI has it
  • If the face changes, go back to the master and try again
  • Don't let the AI change clothes or background

✅ If You're Ever Lost

Remember the rule:

All seven shots must look like they were filmed on the same day, with the same actors, in the same room, with the same camera.
